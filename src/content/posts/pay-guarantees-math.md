The banner says "Earn at least $220 for 20 trips this weekend." It reads like a bonus. It is not a bonus. It's a floor — and the whole trick is that the floor is usually set just below where you'd have landed anyway.

Here's the mechanic nobody spells out. A guarantee doesn't add money on top of your fares. It tops you *up* to the guaranteed number only if your normal earnings fall short. Do 20 trips and earn $240 in fares? You get $240 — the guarantee pays you nothing. Do 20 trips and earn $210? The app adds $10 to hit $220. The guarantee only ever activates when you were already having a below-average day.

So the real question isn't "can I hit the guarantee." It's "is the guarantee set high enough to ever pay out." And the answer, for a normal weekend in a normal market, is usually no.

**Run the numbers once and you stop chasing them:**

- Take your guarantee and divide by the trip requirement. `$220 / 20 = $11 per trip`. Now ask: do you already average more than $11 a trip? If yes, the guarantee is decoration. It will never trigger.
- Guarantees quietly change what you optimize for. To hit "20 trips" you start accepting short, cheap rides you'd normally skip — which drags your per-trip average *down*, toward the guarantee, which is exactly the behavior that makes the floor look relevant. The requirement engineers the outcome.
- The time window is the catch. "This weekend" means those 20 trips have to happen in a set span, or none of it counts. Miss trip 20 by one and the whole thing pays zero. A partial guarantee is not a partial payout. It's nothing.

None of this is fraud. It's just a promotion designed by people who know your average earnings better than you do, priced so it costs them very little to offer. That's not a conspiracy — it's a spreadsheet. The fix is to have your own spreadsheet.

Track your real per-trip and per-hour numbers for two weeks. Once you know them, every guarantee banner sorts instantly into one of two piles: "this actually pays me more than my baseline" (rare, take it) or "this is nudging me to work cheaper for the same money" (common, ignore it). The banner is designed to feel like the first pile. The math tells you which one it really is.

Vibes say the guarantee is free money. Numbers say it's a floor you were already standing on.
